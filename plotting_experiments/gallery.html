1. CLAM Matrix Heatmaps

The Cluster Labels Alignment Matrix (CLAM) is the fundamental output of the ERICA analysis. Each row represents a sample, each column a cluster, and each entry the number of times that sample was assigned to that cluster across B = 200 Monte Carlo train/test iterations. Sorting rows by primary cluster assignment reveals the block-diagonal structure characteristic of stable clusterings.

Figure 1a
Sorted CLAM Heatmap — VDX Breast Cancer, K-Means, K=3
Samples sorted by primary cluster assignment (argmax), then by assignment confidence (descending) within each cluster. Clean diagonal blocks indicate stable cluster membership. Off-diagonal entries reveal samples that oscillate between clusters across iterations. VDX data: 344 samples, 3 genes (ESR1, ERBB2, AURKA) from Parmigiani et al.
ERICA(data, method='kmeans', k_range=[2..6], n_iterations=200) → get_results()['clam_matrices'][(3, 'kmeans')]
Figure 1b
Unsorted CLAM Heatmap — VDX Breast Cancer, K-Means, K=3
Same data as Figure 1a but with samples in their original order. Without sorting, the block structure is obscured. Comparison with the sorted view demonstrates the value of the CLAM sort as a diagnostic tool.
Figure 1c
CLAM Evolution Across K — VDX Breast Cancer, K-Means
Sorted CLAM heatmaps at K=2, 3, and 4 (left to right). As K increases, existing clusters subdivide. At K=2, two clean blocks. At K=3, a third group emerges from the larger cluster. At K=4, further fragmentation with less stable assignments (more off-diagonal entries). This progression informs optimal K selection: the K at which blocks remain clean is the last "real" division.
Figure 1d
HDBSCAN CLAM — VDX Breast Cancer, Modal K=2
HDBSCAN discovers K automatically via density-based clustering. Across 200 iterations, the modal K was 2 with 60% agreement (121/200 iterations). The CLAM matrix is constructed only from iterations where HDBSCAN found K=2. Noise points (label = -1) are reassigned to the nearest cluster centroid before CLAM computation.
get_auto_k_results('hdbscan')['clam_matrix'] — shape (344, 2)

2. Sample Stability Profiles

Stability strip plots normalize each sample's CLAM row to proportions and display them as horizontal stacked bars. Samples are sorted by Shannon entropy: perfectly stable samples (entropy = 0, always assigned to one cluster) appear at the top; maximally confused samples (entropy ≈ log2K) appear at the bottom.

Figure 2a
Stability Strips — VDX, K-Means, K=3
344 samples (subsampled to 100 for display). Upper samples are unambiguously assigned; lower samples split between two clusters. The transition zone reveals the boundary population — samples whose cluster identity depends on which training subsample was drawn.
Figure 2b
Stability Strips — VDX, Ward Linkage, K=3
Same dataset, different method. Ward linkage produces a longer "confused" tail compared to K-Means, suggesting its cluster boundaries are more sensitive to the particular training subsample. This is reflected in Ward's lower CRI at K=3 (0.938 vs 0.975 for K-Means).

3. Replicability Metric Curves

Two families of metrics quantify clustering replicability. ERICA metrics (CRI, WCRI, TWCRI) measure assignment consistency from the CLAM matrix. Parmigiani metrics (ARI, AMI) measure partition agreement between train-fitted and test-fitted clusterings. Both are computed per K; the optimal K* is selected by the non-decreasing TWCRI criterion (Algorithm 2).

Figure 3a
Metrics vs K — VDX, K-Means
Left panel: CRI (blue), WCRI (vermillion), TWCRI (green) vs K=2..6. All three metrics peak at K=2 then decline, but the non-decreasing criterion selects K*=6 because TWCRI recovers at K=6. Vertical dashed lines mark K* per metric. Right panel: ARI and AMI (mean ± std across 200 iterations). ARI declines monotonically from K=2, reflecting increasing difficulty of reproducing finer partitions on held-out data. Error bars show iteration-to-iteration variance.
metrics[k]['kmeans']['CRI'], ['ARI_mean'], ['ARI_std'] — K*: k_star['TWCRI']['kmeans']
Figure 3b
Well-Separated Blobs — K-Means
300 samples, 5 features, 3 true clusters (std=0.8). CRI/TWCRI = 1.000 at K=3 with ARI = 1.000 ± 0.000. K=4 and K=5 are disqualified (NaN, marked with ×) due to empty clusters. K*=3, perfectly matching the ground truth.
Figure 3c
Overlapping Blobs — K-Means
300 samples, 5 features, 4 true clusters (std=2.0). TWCRI peaks at K=4 (0.979), correctly identifying the true K despite substantial cluster overlap. ARI = 0.952 ± 0.121 at K=4 — high mean but wide variance reflecting the difficulty of the boundary cases.

4. Method Comparison

ERICA supports multiple clustering methods in a single run: K-Means, Agglomerative (Ward and Single linkage), and HDBSCAN. Comparing methods on the same data reveals which aspects of cluster structure each method captures — and which it misses.

Figure 4a
Method Comparison — VDX
CRI, WCRI, TWCRI for K-Means (orange) and Ward (blue) across K=2..6. HDBSCAN appears as a star marker at its modal K=2. K-Means consistently outperforms Ward on ERICA metrics for this dataset, suggesting more stable centroid-based boundaries for these three genes.
Figure 4b
K* Selection — VDX
Grouped bar chart of K* per method per metric. Both K-Means and Ward select K*=6 across all three ERICA metrics, suggesting the K range should be extended to test larger values. The consistent K*=6 at the range boundary is a signal, not an answer.

5. Cluster Assignment Scatter Plots

For two-dimensional datasets, we can directly visualize cluster assignments in the feature space. Points are colored by their primary CLAM cluster (argmax of each row). Point size scales with assignment confidence: larger dots were more consistently assigned across iterations. Confidence maps (green = stable, red = confused) highlight the boundary regions.

Key finding: K-Means selected K*=4 for the moons dataset, splitting each crescent in half. This is not a bug — it is K-Means being honest about its geometric assumptions. Centroid-based methods cannot represent non-convex clusters. Ward linkage (K*=2) and HDBSCAN (modal K=2, 93% agreement) correctly identify the two-moon structure.
Figure 5a
Method Comparison at K=2 — Two Moons
300 samples from make_moons(noise=0.05). Left: K-Means draws a vertical decision boundary, splitting each moon. Center: Ward linkage does the same. Right: HDBSCAN separates the moons correctly. Point sizes reflect CLAM confidence — note the smaller (less confident) points near the inter-moon gap across all methods.
Figure 5b
K-Means at K*=4 — Moons
K-Means' selected K*=4. Each crescent is bisected into two segments. The TWCRI is higher at K=4 than K=3 because the four-piece partition is actually more stable than the three-piece one — but it doesn't match the generative structure.
Figure 5c
Confidence Map — Moons, K-Means, K=2
Green = high confidence (always same cluster), red = low confidence. The inter-moon boundary lights up red. The tips of each crescent, farthest from the boundary, are the most stable. This confidence gradient is a direct readout of the CLAM matrix normalized by row sums.
Figure 5d
Method Comparison at K=2 — Concentric Circles
300 samples from make_circles(noise=0.05, factor=0.5). All methods struggle: K-Means and Ward split vertically (ignoring the radial structure), HDBSCAN finds 4 clusters with only 39% agreement. Concentric rings require topology-aware methods that none of these algorithms provide. The uniformly low CRI (~0.78) across methods correctly flags this as an unreliable clustering.
Figure 5e
Method Comparison at K=3 — Well-Separated 2D Blobs
The control case. 300 samples, 3 isotropic Gaussian clusters (std=0.5). All three methods produce identical assignments with perfect confidence. CRI = 1.0, ARI = 1.0. When the clusters are real, every method finds them.

6. K-Value Progression Series

The definitive view: how cluster assignments evolve as K increases from 2 to 6. Each grid has 3 rows (K-Means, Ward, Single Linkage) and 5 columns (K=2 through K=6). For 2D data, cells show scatter plots; for higher-dimensional data, cells show sorted CLAM heatmaps. Orange borders mark the K* selected by TWCRI. True K is marked with an asterisk in the column header.

Figure 6a
K Progression — Two Moons (true K=2)
K-Means: progressively chops the moons into more pieces, selecting K*=4. Ward: similar fragmentation pattern, but selects K*=2 (correct). Single Linkage: correctly identifies 2 clusters at K=2 by following the density chain. At K≥3, single linkage begins isolating small outlier groups. Point sizes reflect CLAM confidence.
Figure 6b
K Progression — Concentric Circles (true K=2)
No method correctly separates inner from outer ring at any K. All methods default to spatial bisection. As K increases, the circular structure is carved into pie-slice segments rather than concentric regions. This is a fundamental limitation of distance-based and linkage-based clustering for non-radial topology.
Figure 6c
K Progression — VDX Breast Cancer (true K unknown)
CLAM heatmaps for real data. At K=2, clean two-block structure across all methods. At K=3, a third block emerges but with visible off-diagonal assignments. Single linkage produces a degenerate partition at K≥3 (one giant cluster + tiny fragments). K-Means and Ward show the most interpretable progression, with block structure degrading gradually as K increases.
Figure 6d
K Progression — Anisotropic Gaussian Mixture (true K=3)
Three elongated Gaussians at different orientations in 2D (450 samples). K-Means selects K*=4, splitting an elongated cluster. Ward selects K*=2, merging two clusters. Single Linkage selects K*=3 (correct), following the chain structure of each elongated component. Demonstrates that method selection should be informed by expected cluster geometry.
Figure 6e
K Progression — Five-Cluster GMM (true K=5)
Five Gaussians with varied covariances in 2D (400 samples). K-Means and Ward both reach K*=5 (correct). Single Linkage oversplits to K*=7, fragmented by its sensitivity to density variations between clusters. At K=5, all K-based methods show clean five-way assignments.

8. Gaussian Mixture Sigma Study

The central experiment: 4 Gaussian centers in 50 dimensions, with the standard deviation swept from 0.01 (trivially separated) to 10.0 (indistinguishable). This recapitulates the original MCSS synthetic experiments and validates ERICA's behavior across the full spectrum of clustering difficulty. Adjacent centers are separated by 9.49 in Euclidean distance; the expected point-to-center distance is σ√d.

The separation equation: At σ=0.01, the radius/spacing ratio is 0.01 (perfect separation). At σ=1.0, it reaches 0.75 (severe overlap). At σ=10.0, it hits 7.45 (complete collapse). ERICA's metrics track this transition precisely.
Figure 8a
TWCRI at True K=4 vs Standard Deviation
The "replicability cliff." TWCRI = 1.0 at σ ≤ 0.1 (clusters are trivially separated). At σ=1.0, methods diverge: K-Means and Ward maintain moderate TWCRI while Single Linkage drops. At σ=10.0, all methods collapse. HDBSCAN (diamonds) tracks the trend but operates at its own modal K, which shifts from 4 to 1 as structure vanishes.
Figure 8b
ARI/AMI at K=4 vs Sigma
Parmigiani metrics tell a complementary story. ARI (solid) and AMI (dashed) both show perfect train-to-test generalization at low sigma. The ARI/AMI divergence at σ=1.0 reflects information-theoretic vs pair-counting sensitivity to partial overlap.
Figure 8c
HDBSCAN Noise Fraction vs Sigma
The fraction of test samples classified as noise by HDBSCAN. At σ ≤ 0.1, zero noise. As sigma increases, HDBSCAN progressively classifies more samples as noise — its way of saying "this isn't a cluster." At σ=10.0, most samples are noise. HDBSCAN as a canary for cluster structure.
Figure 8d
K* Selection Across Sigma Levels
At σ ≤ 0.1, all methods agree: K*=4 (the truth). At σ=1.0, K-Means and Ward still find K*=4 but Single Linkage diverges to K*=6 (oversplitting the overlapping clusters). At σ=10.0, all methods select K*=2 (the two closest centers merge last) and HDBSCAN finds modal K=1. The horizontal reference line marks the true K=4.
Figure 8e
VDX vs Gaussian (σ=1.0) Comparison
Where does real breast cancer data fall on the synthetic difficulty spectrum? Side-by-side metric curves for VDX (left) and the σ=1.0 Gaussian (right). VDX shows higher absolute TWCRI but a similar declining pattern with K, suggesting its cluster structure is present but not dramatically separated — consistent with biological heterogeneity rather than clean molecular subtypes.

K Progression by Sigma Level

Figure 8f
σ = 0.01 — Point Masses
Razor-sharp CLAM blocks. Every sample is assigned to the same cluster in all 200 iterations. At K=4, four identical-height blocks. At K > 4, empty clusters appear (disqualified). The platonic ideal of replicable clustering.
Figure 8g
σ = 0.1 — Well-Separated
Still clean blocks, now with barely visible off-diagonal fuzz. Expected radius (0.71) is far below the center spacing (9.49). The first hints of boundary uncertainty appear at K=5 and K=6.
Figure 8h
σ = 1.0 — Severe Overlap
The interesting case. Expected radius (7.07) approaches spacing (9.49). K-Means and Ward still show block structure at K=4 but with substantial off-diagonal leakage. Single Linkage produces a degenerate partition: one giant cluster absorbs most samples via the chaining effect. This is where method choice matters most.
Figure 8i
σ = 10.0 — Total Collapse
Expected radius (70.71) dwarfs spacing (9.49). All 400 samples form a single diffuse cloud. K-Means and Ward still impose partitions (they must), but the CLAM heatmaps show no block structure — assignments are essentially random. ERICA correctly reports TWCRI near zero. The most important thing a replicability tool can do is tell you when there's nothing to replicate.
